HISTORY OF SPEECH

“On The Road To Belleville” is the title of an inspirational speech, given on March 3rd, 2014, by Professor Lawrence Brothwilde.
The event was organized by a now-defunct RSO called “Students For A More Vivid Campus” (SFAMVC, an acronym specifically written in the bylaws, even though it can’t really be pronounced.)
It drew an audience of over 300 students, and had a remarkably lasting impact, with students discussing it on public Facebook forums for weeks afterwards. “The Road To Belleville” was even mentioned in a student commencement speech, and the crowd erupted in cheers at the speech’s name.
However, the speech was never filmed in its entirety. All we have are a few phone camera recordings--which are largely useless-- and a transcription of the speech that is considered to be more or less accurate.
The speech details a car trip Professor Brothwilde once took from Chicago (specifically Hyde Park) to Belleville, a town in Essex County, New Jersey.
The speech, at first, appears to be a typical sort of pandering, inspirational thing about appreciating where you come from and where you live, but the meat of the speech points to something more difficult to pin down, and, arguably, far more meaningful.
Later, we discovered that many of the events in “The Road to Belleville” were likely fabricated, particularly since it appears that it was essentially impossible for Brothwilde to have taken a road trip from Hyde Park to Belleville when he said he did. In the only statement he released commenting on the situation, Brothwilde said, “Those memories came from somewhere, didn’t they?”

EXAMINE EXCERPTS

A preliminary note: all excerpts are directly quoted from the speech, unless otherwise noted.
Another note: Some accounts disagree on which form of the speech is the correct one. Discrepancies in the “scholia” are noted; you can choose which options you believe to be correct.
My Belleville is steeped in Chicago, but it is still the same Belleville as everyone else’s. And when I return to Chicago, a part of that Belleville comes with me, even if it is an older one or a misshapen one, or one that is not immediately recognizable.
Small pieces of Belleville and Chicago, such as where things are, how to get to them--they stay behind with me no matter how far away the places themselves are from me. A certain window or small island of grass will remind me of somewhere far away. This does not matter to either place, but here these things both are within me. There are always reasons, in layers that have built up over time, for bits of these places to stay with me instead of remaining where they are.
You might not see Belleville when you see that island of grass, but we’ll still both of us be sharing a place here, in Chicago. And that is what I really mean to talk about today. Nobody really cares about Belleville unless you have a reason to. But this, too, is true of Hyde Park. We all have our own reasons to be here in this place, which is, for all of us, the same place. All of us in a place experience it at the same time. But because of these layers, these strata, we all of us carry, we must also acknowledge the individual meaning we must mine from the place we share.

“‘ ...there are no miracles in the dream other than life; the only way out of the dream is to awaken into death.’” --Danilo Kiš, “Legend of the Sleepers”

I stopped to eat my packed lunch of tuna fish / of roast beef / of Caesar salad / of leftover pizza / of leftover Kung Pao chicken at a rest stop at some place in between where I was going and where I had started. I never learned what state it was in, although if I tried to find it, I’m sure I could. / It was in West Virgina, but I’m not sure where. / It was, to be precise, the Grover Cleveland rest stop on the New Jersey Turnpike. But this space in between Hyde Park and Belleville is perhaps more real, even, than either of those places when I am away from both of them. But it was in a place with mountains / streams / tangled highways / smokestacks / a grand view , and the road opened up and I stopped for far longer than I needed to, even though I was almost home / I was low on gas / I was lost / none of these . Standing there, I was struck with a happiness so simple and so complete that I have yet to find a fair way of putting it into words.

Dr. Lawrence Brothwilde is Professor Emeritus in the Slavic Languages and Literatures department.
Much of his work has focused on the mythic and the unreal in 20th-century literature, primarily in eastern Europe but also in the Soviet Union. His most famous works are books on Danilo Kiš and Andrey Platonov, but both books are notoriously difficult to find.
He has delivered a number of stirring lectures over his 37 years as a professor at the University.
He famously prohibits recordings or copies of his speeches.
Brothwilde was born in Belleville, NJ, in 1938, and lived there until he graduated from high school. He then received his BA in Slavic Languages and Literature and his SB in Mathematics from Princeton. Brothwilde then moved to UChicago for graduate school, and, has in his own words, “essentially never left Hyde Park after that.”
He still holds an office in the Harper West tower; secondhand reports say that the office has many miniature mobiles hanging from the ceiling, and is papered with various posters (many of which involve boxing matches and Clint Eastwood movies.) He also has an autographed picture of the band My Chemical Romance, whose members also hail from Belleville.
Dr. Brothwilde also has a substantial desktop computer in his office, that many say looks suspiciously like a gaming rig.
